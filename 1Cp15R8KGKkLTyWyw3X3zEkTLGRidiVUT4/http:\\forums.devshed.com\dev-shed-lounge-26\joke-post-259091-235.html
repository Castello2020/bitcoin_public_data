    So, a guy moves out to the sticks...

    ...because he's just tired of all the noise and crowding in the big city.

    He's there for a few weeks and is getting a little stir crazy. One day, he hears a knock at his door. Parting the curtain, he sees a man standing on his porch.

    Our guy opens the door, and the man on the porch says, "I thought someone moved in here, but I haven't seen you around, so I thought I would come by and say hi."

    Excited, our guy invites him in.

    The man says, "Listen, I'm having a party. Would you like to come?"

    Our guy says, "You bet!"

    The man says, "Okay look, we do things a little different out here. Now, do you like to drink?"

    Our guy says, "I love to drink!"

    "And do you like to dance?" the man asks.

    "Dancing's great fun!"

    The man gives him the once over, and says, "Sometimes these parties can get a little rough. Can you use your hands?"

    Undaunted, the man says, "I can protect myself, if that's what you're asking."

    "Alright. Well, there's bound to be some blood, some crying, and maybe even some sex. What do you think of that?"

    Our guy is so lonely, he's bound to endure anything to socialize. Finally he says, "Okay, I'm in."

    The man says, "Be at my place, down the road on the left, on Saturday night, around eight," and turns to leave the house.

    Our guy says, "Wait, what should I wear?"

    The man says, "Oh, I don't care. It's just gonna be you and me."

    Code:
    What are they thinking?

Integrating with Xbox, Kinect, and Windows Phone is the sum total of the plan that Microsoft put in the press release. At the press conference there was also some non-specific talk about advertising. But that's it. That's Microsoft's grand vision for its $8.5 billion purchase. Adding voice and video chat to its games console and phone platform and maybe showing a few ads.

Never mind that the company has its own voice and video chat platform already. Never mind that it shows ads on that platform already. Never mind that its own platform is bigger than Skype's�Windows Live Messenger boasts in excess of 330 million monthly active users with 40 million concurrent, compared to Skype's 170 million and 20 million-30 million concurrent. Never mind that it already plans to integrate Windows Live Messenger into Windows Phone, and surely has the development expertise to integrate Messenger into Xbox and Kinect for a fraction of the $8.5 billion it's spending for Skype. Never mind that Messenger already interoperates with Lync. Never mind that Messenger already integrates with Office. All this was forgotten or ignored.
