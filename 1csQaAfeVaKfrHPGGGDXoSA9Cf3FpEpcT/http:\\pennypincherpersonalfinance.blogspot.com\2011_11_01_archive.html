Habeas what corpus?

Let them enter the hall of shame.
These senators today voted to indefinitely suspend habeas corpus, as part of an amendment to the Defense Authorization Act.  Not only that, but this bill, the way they passed it, would allow them to send Americans to Gitmo indefinitely with no trial.

At first blush it would seem the opposite.  There is a provision in the bill saying it doesn't apply to Americans  Here is the text of that part.

SEC. 1032. REQUIREMENT FOR MILITARY CUSTODY.

    (a) Custody Pending Disposition Under Law of War-
      (1) IN GENERAL- Except as provided in paragraph (4), the Armed Forces of the United States shall hold a person described in paragraph (2) who is captured in the course of hostilities authorized by the Authorization for Use of Military Force (Public Law 107-40) in military custody pending disposition under the law of war.
      (2) COVERED PERSONS- The requirement in paragraph (1) shall apply to any person whose detention is authorized under section 1031 who is determined--
        (A) to be a member of, or part of, al-Qaeda or an associated force that acts in coordination with or pursuant to the direction of al-Qaeda; and
        (B) to have participated in the course of planning or carrying out an attack or attempted attack against the United States or its coalition partners.
      (3) DISPOSITION UNDER LAW OF WAR- For purposes of this subsection, the disposition of a person under the law of war has the meaning given in section 1031(c), except that no transfer otherwise described in paragraph (4) of that section shall be made unless consistent with the requirements of section 1033.
      (4) WAIVER FOR NATIONAL SECURITY- The Secretary of Defense may, in consultation with the Secretary of State and the Director of National Intelligence, waive the requirement of paragraph (1) if the Secretary submits to Congress a certification in writing that such a waiver is in the national security interests of the United States.
    (b) Applicability to United States Citizens and Lawful Resident Aliens-
      (1) UNITED STATES CITIZENS- The requirement to detain a person in military custody under this section does not extend to citizens of the United States.
      (2) LAWFUL RESIDENT ALIENS- The requirement to detain a person in military custody under this section does not extend to a lawful resident alien of the United States on the basis of conduct taking place within the United States, except to the extent permitted by the Constitution of the United States.

    Smart Meters Not So Smart

    There is a push on to get residential consumers of electricity (that's you) to switch over to "smart meters".  This is supposed to "save money".  But it's not you who will save money.

    Currently, the "old" meters don't measure peaks, like if you need more power for a second to start up a motor, for example.  Also they are only read once a month, so if you have a party one weekend and you're out of town the next weekend, it evens out.

    The new meters measure peaks and they are also read more like weekly.  If you used more power one week, the power company would bill you on a higher usage tier, and possibly apply it to the entire month.  This means an increase in your electric bill of 5 to 30 percent. 

    Smart meters are designed to be read from afar, in other words they broadcast their readings.  They can turn off your power remotely as well, not having to send a guy out.  Because they broadcast all the time, the meters emit electromagnetic radiation, which can make you sick if you get too much of it.  Think about the poor guy living in the apartment of a complex where all the meters for the whole complex are just outside his bedroom wall.  Some people are very sensitive to EMF and are bothered more by it than others, but all people get sick eventually from it - cancer, etc.  It can also cause mental disturbances.  The toll to public health (and the cost thereof) could exceed any "savings" to energy use caused by the changes in the consumer's behavior when they start getting those big electric bills.  It can also make your pets and children sick.

    Large war-technology manufacturers such as Lockheed-Martin, Boeing, SAIC, Raytheon, and BAE are trying to get big government bids to make and install smart meters in homes all over the US.  There is a lot of pork for this.  The feds approved some of these bids before the safety studies were done. Not only the meters themselves, but the cybersecurity for the meters is a cash cow for these guys.

    I might also point out that the smart meters also make it so that the federal government, or their privatized defense contractors, will have jurisdiction over how much electricity you are allowed to have, and they could also attempt to harass you by turning off your electricity from afar.

    You can refuse a smart meter, but you might not even notice they've put one in.  They might just do it and not ask.  If you discover you have one and you don't want it, you can ask that they put the old one back in, but there is usually a fee involved of a few hundred dollars.

    So the smart meters are basically a way to keep the war manufacturers working, while passing the cost on to consumers, and they also give out more granular information about how you use the energy in your home.  Plus, they're a lot more hackable than the old meters, despite all the cybersecurity they'll be getting.  Some hacker could much more easily knock out parts of the grid under the new smart meters.

    Fake Software Updates Could Be Spyware

    From the Wall Street Journal

    They are selling this software to law enforcement.

    I would just assume you're being spied on, and keep your private communication as unelectronic as possible.  i.e. take the battery out of your cell phone, or leave it behind, etc. and just have an in-person conversation far from street lamps, cars, buildings, etc.  Then all you have to worry about is if the person you are speaking to is wired, or if there's someone else or some battery powered surveillance equipment hiding in the bushes nearby.

    For when you can't avoid the electronics, you can encrypt stuff, but if there's a key logger on your computer, encryption won't do you much good.  In one of your unelectronic moments you could agree on a few code words with your friends.  Or you could switch to Linux on a different computer for all your private stuff, and you might get around a lot of that spyware crap.  Especially if you are running your OS from a CD-Rom instead of the hard drive.  That way every time you boot up, you've got the original OS with no updates or other moss it might have gathered.  I recommend SLAX.     When in college, for a class on women and religion in late antiquity, I wrote a paper comparing witchcraft from late antiquity to the middle ages to modern times.  For the older source material I enjoyed Apuleius' "The Golden Ass", which is a late antiquity novel about a guy who gets turned into an ass by a witch, and his adventures trying to get back to human form.  Apuleius was also at one point himself accused of witchcraft, and represented himself pro se in court.  He was acquitted.  His speech that he gave in court was preserved for posterity, and is very funny.  I also read translations of items found in archaeological digs that were related to magic spells cast by ordinary ancient Greeks and Romans who had resorted to magic.  For more modern times I just used popular culture as it relates to the typical Halloween witch; also some Shakespeare.  I wasn't trying to drag the Wiccans into it.

    I found that certain things have remained the same about the popular (i.e. literary) idea of witches dating back to before Christianity appeared on the scene: Shapeshifting or causing others to turn into animals, the use of other people's hair in spells, sexual shenanigans, prayers to whoever was the current god of the underworld, and the preparation of brews using yucky ingredients.
