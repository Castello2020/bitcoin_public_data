Jerusalem Artichokes Perfect for Guerrilla Gardening

credit: Wikimedia Commons
 I'm on my 2nd year of growing Jerusalem artichokes.  I grew one last year as an experiment and dug it out after it overwintered.  I must have missed some tubers or maybe the seeds took, because this year I have a patch of them.  They are competing with my mint to see who can dominate the bishops weed. (all very aggressive plants).  This is a plant related to the sunflower, but with a lot of 2-inch diameter yellow flowers that resemble black-eyed Susans.

The Jerusalem artichoke is neither an artichoke nor from Jerusalem.  It's native to the US.

I was doing some reading to find out when to harvest these things, and if I want tubers instead of forage for cows, then it'll have to be in November at the earliest, and I don't want to cut the stems off before then.  The interesting thing, though, is if I refrigerate these tubers, the mostly inulin starch will change to fructose.  This will make them sweet and less farty. (Inulin makes you fart, like beans) I might even be able to brew alcohol from them. Or I can dehydrate them for my pantry.  I had thought to get some sugar beet seeds, but maybe it won't be necessary, although it would be nice to have both.

Jerusalem artichokes are a weed.  They don't have many diseases, and they stand up good to stem borer bugs. i.e. the bugs will try but they won't kill the plants.  As a weed, they'd be a perfect plant to guerrilla garden on the sides of roads where they don't mow.  When the SHTF maybe they won't mow as much.  If they do get mowed down, the tubers will still survive, but be smaller.  It would be a way to still have sugar even if they ban it, or if the movement of goods is so restricted that cane sugar can't come up north.

Now I need to find a northern source of caffeine and I'll be all set.

Ebola Outbreak in Uganda Reaches Capital

http://www.zerohedge.com/news/uganda-ebola-outbreak-reaches-capital

Ebola virus is the most virulent disease in the world.  It is a hemorrhagic fever, and is both an infectious and a contagious disease (in other words you can get it by touch, and by breathing if they cough on you).  It makes your blood cells explode, basically, although that's not a real technical description.  Symptoms include fever, aches and pains, vomiting, diarrhea, and blood in your bodily secretions.  Mostly it spreads by contact with bodily secretions of someone who has it.  It takes only a few days to die, and it's around 55% fatal.  Also the disease develops quickly in people who are exposed to it. (short incubation period).

 There are 4 strains of ebola, all from Africa, and apparently nobody knows how it came into being.  It showed up in the 1970's.  The symptoms are just like Marburg disease and also resemble a lot of other tropical diseases, so testing is important to know what it is.

There is a vaccine for ebola but it's a series of them and it takes 6 months to complete the course of vaccines, so an outbreak is too late to start with it if you're trying to avoid that outbreak.

The good news is that this disease only tends to turn epidemic in places with horrible sanitation, where people are sharing needles and not sterilizing them and that sort of thing.  Places with decent hospitals, that know to isolate patients and wear gowns and gloves and masks while handling the patients, don't have epidemics of ebola so much.  So we probably don't need to worry about an ebola outbreak jumping to the US through an airport and becoming an epidemic.  If it did, not too many people would get sick before they had it under control.

The government of Uganda is telling people not to touch each other, kiss or have sex, and if they have a relative that they think died of ebola, to not bury them themselves but call for health workers to do it.  The bodily fluids of the dead are still contagious.
